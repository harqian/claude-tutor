Why $E = 0$ inside a conductor at equilibrium

Step through the three phases. Blue dots are free electrons; orange dots are fixed lattice ions.

fixed + ions free − electrons Gaussian surface applied field
Neutral bulk: free electrons are paired with ions throughout the whole interior, not just the surface. Any interior Gaussian surface encloses zero net charge: $q_\text{enc} = 0$, so $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = 0$.
Apply $E$ rightward: free electrons throughout the bulk feel $\vec{F} = q\vec{E}$ and begin drifting left. This is not equilibrium — the charges are still accelerating. The system keeps adjusting until the internal field is canceled.
Equilibrium: electrons have shifted left, exposing positive ions on the right. The induced surface charge creates an equal and opposite internal $\vec{E}$. Net result: $\vec{E} = 0$ everywhere inside. Gauss confirms: $q_\text{enc} = 0$ for any interior surface, so all net charge lives on the outer surface only.

The argument

  1. Conductor: free electrons present throughout the bulk, one per lattice ion, everywhere.
  2. Equilibrium: nothing accelerating $\Rightarrow$ $\vec{F} = q\vec{E} = 0$ on every free electron $\Rightarrow$ $\vec{E}_\text{inside} = 0$.
  3. Gauss on any interior surface: $E = 0$ on it $\Rightarrow$ $\oint\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = 0$ $\Rightarrow$ $q_\text{enc} = 0$ $\Rightarrow$ net charge on surface only.

Chain: free charges everywhere + equilibrium $\Rightarrow$ $E=0$ inside $\Rightarrow$ Gauss $\Rightarrow$ net charge on surface only.
