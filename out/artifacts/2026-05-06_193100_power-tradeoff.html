Statistical power, in two overlapping curves

You're testing $H_0: \mu = 0$ against $H_a: \mu > 0$ at significance level α. There are two possible worlds for the truth and two possible decisions you can make. The whole α / β / power story is just where the curves overlap and where the cutoff sits.

The blue curve is the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ if $H_0$ is true (centered at 0). The orange curve is the sampling distribution if the true mean is $\mu_a$. Both have spread $\text{SE} = \sigma/\sqrt{n}$.

The dashed vertical line is the cutoff $c$ — drag it left or right. You reject $H_0$ when $\bar{x} > c$. Three colored regions:

  • α — right tail of the blue (null) curve past $c$. The probability of false alarm: rejecting when $H_0$ is actually true.
  • β — left tail of the orange (alt) curve before $c$. The probability of missing it: failing to reject when there really is an effect.
  • power = 1 − β — right tail of the orange (alt) curve past $c$. The probability of correctly catching a real effect.

Try this:

1. Drag the cutoff right. Watch α shrink and β grow. They move opposite at fixed design — that's the tradeoff.

2. Drag the orange curve's mean rightward (or use the effect-size slider). Bigger effect → curves further apart → less overlap → power up.

3. Crank $n$ up to 100. Both curves become narrow spikes. Even at α = 0.05, power approaches 1. This is why "more data" is the most reliable lever.

4. Set $n = 5$ and $\mu_a = 0.5$. See how power collapses below 0.2. This is what underpowered studies look like — they almost never detect the effect, even when it's real.

Why $\alpha + \beta \neq 1$

Each curve integrates to 1 separately. α lives entirely under the blue curve; β lives entirely under the orange curve. They're conditional probabilities on different conditions ($H_0$ true vs $H_a$ true), so adding them is a category error. The real relationship is a curve in α-β space, traced as you slide $c$.
