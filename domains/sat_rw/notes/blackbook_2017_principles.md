# Black Book principles for SAT R&W

A distilled principle layer. Skipped: per-question walkthroughs, the SAT essay (gone in digital), and the full grammar toolbox (those are facts to memorize, not principles).

## I. The single biggest principle (the whole book hangs on this)

**Every correct answer is restated or demonstrated *somewhere on the page*. No interpretation, ever.**

- "Restated" = same idea, different words.
- "Demonstrated" = the text shows an example/scenario that fits the answer choice (or the answer choice fits an example/scenario in the text).
- The College Board *must* engineer questions this way, because a multiple-choice test with millions of takers cannot survive subjective grading.
- Corollary: if you find yourself "interpreting," "inferring tone," or "guessing what the author would think" — stop. The answer is on the page; you haven't located it yet.

**Why most students never notice this:**
1. They aren't looking for it (they read like an English class wants them to).
2. Question prompts use words like *primarily, suggests, likely, most nearly* — engineered to *sound* subjective. Mentally rewrite as *"which is the only choice that..."*
3. They aren't precise enough about exact word meanings. "Socially beneficial" ≠ "beneficial to society." One word wrong = wrong answer.
4. They sometimes hold a flawed definition of a word and don't notice.

## II. The 7 wrong-answer patterns (memorize)

Don't classify wrong answers on test day; just *know what tricks exist* so you don't fall for them.

1. **Off by one or two words.** Mirrors text exactly except for one swapped word. Most dangerous — beats students who already know the rules.
2. **Barely relevant.** Vague, doesn't contradict anything, just isn't supported. Tempting because it doesn't *feel* wrong.
3. **Valid literary interpretation.** What an English teacher would accept. Symbolism, character motive, theme. Wrong on the SAT.
4. **Reasonable but not in the text.** True-sounding outside knowledge. Especially dangerous on science/history passages.
5. **Direct contradiction.** Says the opposite of the text. Designed to make you doubt yourself and waste time re-reading.
6. **Confused relationships.** Uses the right concepts but scrambles their connections (who did what to whom).
7. **Restates the wrong part of the passage.** Accurate restatement, but of a portion that isn't what the question is asking about.

**The unifying frame:** wrong answers all fail to restate/demonstrate the *relevant* part of the text, or smuggle in ideas that aren't on the page.

## III. Reading process (5 steps)

1. **Read the passage introduction in full.** It's not optional. Some questions hinge on author identity / publication year / context.
2. **Read, skim, or skip the passage** — your choice. Three valid styles:
   - **Read it once.** Don't take notes. Don't re-read for "deep understanding."
   - **Skip the passage; do citation questions first.** Use line numbers as a map, then mop up no-citation questions afterward.
   - **Skim for a mental map** of where concepts live. Don't try to understand — just locate.
3. **Read the question. Note the citation if there is one. Read only the relevant text** (and 1–2 sentences before/after if the citation cuts a sentence).
4. **Find three wrong answers.** Easier than confirming one right. If you can't eliminate three, you've misread something. If you eliminate all four, ditto.
5. **Confirm the remaining choice fits the restate/demonstrate pattern.**

**Don't take notes.** Note-taking forces interpretation, which is the failure mode.

## IV. Reading sub-tactics

- **Bad connection approach.** When a sentence/phrase doesn't make sense, don't panic — work from what *does* make sense around it. Treat reading like a glitchy phone call: bits and pieces are usually enough.
- **Easiest-questions-first** within and across passages. Don't stereotype passages as hard.
- **Recognize unknown words/slang/cultural references** but don't worry about them unless a question asks.
- **Rhetorical questions** in older passages have obvious/extreme/broad implied answers — that's the function of the device.
- **Comma/dash/parenthesis sandwiches**: mentally remove the sandwiched phrase to parse long sentences. The phrase usually doesn't change the core meaning.
- **Old-text weird punctuation:** ignore it; just read the words.
- **Technical / academic terms** can usually be ignored unless the question references them.

## V. Question-type-specific heuristics

| Type | Heuristic |
|---|---|
| **Most nearly means** | Not "find a synonym." Find the choice that *restates* a phrase actually in the text, or is *demonstrated* by the relevant text. |
| **Best evidence** | The cited line in the right answer is sometimes *adjacent to* (not exactly) the supporting evidence. **Shortcut:** if a "best evidence" question follows a previous question, pair-solve them by checking which evidence-choice supports any answer-choice in the previous question. |
| **Attitude / characterize** | Still literal. The text always directly states how the author feels — find the words. Don't read mood from style. |
| **Purpose** | Don't guess intent. The text demonstrates the purpose; pick the choice that the text plainly demonstrates. |
| **Central claim / main idea** | Pick the choice that restates a quote (or quotes) from the relevant section. Not "vibe of the passage." |
| **Summary / development** | The right answer restates key elements *in sequence* from the cited text. |
| **Humor** | "Humorous" on the SAT means either (a) couldn't be literally true, or (b) unexpected/unusual. Not "made you laugh." |
| **Yes-yes-no-no** | Two halves: yes/no answer + explanation. Attack the explanation half first (it's the literal-restatement filter). Confirm the yes/no fits. |
| **Paired passages** | Question asks "how would author of P1 react to P2?" → P1 must already directly state the relevant view. Find it in P1. |
| **Figure / data** | Match the choice to what the figure literally shows. No interpretation. Wrong choices often contradict the figure or import outside knowledge. |
| **Questions without citations** | Same rules. Answer is still spelled out — you just have to locate it. Do citation questions first; they often illuminate where no-citation answers live. |

## VI. Writing & Language: two big secrets

W&L (digital SAT folds this into the unified R&W section) splits into **two question types** with different rule sets:

### A. Reading-Comprehension-style W&L questions
Treat exactly like Reading. Restate/demonstrate, no interpretation. Slight tilt toward demonstration over restatement.

Sub-types:
- **"Best accomplishes [goal]"** — pick the choice the text plainly demonstrates as fitting the stated goal.
- **Transitions** (however, therefore, furthermore, also, for instance...): pick the word that correctly demonstrates the logical relationship between the previous and current sentence. *"Therefore"* and *"however"* signal opposite logical structures; using the wrong one is wrong even if the sentences sound fine.
- **Sentence/paragraph placement.** Reorder so cross-references resolve in the right direction (a pronoun like *that* or *her* must come after its referent; problem→solution order; introduction before discussion).
- **Vocabulary in context.** Words you know — but precise meanings differ. "Decreed" vs "commissioned" vs "forced" vs "licensed" — they're not interchangeable. Ask: which exactly fits the passage's situation?
- **Include/exclude a sentence (3 rules in order):**
  1. Pick the choice whose *description of the sentence's role* literally matches what the sentence does. Ignore the yes/no part initially.
  2. If two pass rule 1, apply general "ideal paragraph" standards: include relevant details, transitions, intros to next-sentence ideas; exclude irrelevant or unconnected ideas.
  3. Don't include a sentence that contradicts a figure/data in the passage.
- **Figure questions.** Match to what's literally shown.

### B. Grammar / Style / Punctuation
**These follow College Board rules, not modern usage or what your English teacher accepts.** "Sounds right" is unreliable.

Core rules to internalize (non-exhaustive — full toolbox is mechanical):

- **Subject-verb agreement.** Plural noun → plural verb, even when separated by an intervening phrase (`the amounts of residue ... have`, not `has`). Mentally remove intervening phrases.
- **Pronoun number agreement.** Singular noun → singular pronoun. `An antique car ... it was over 100 years old` (not *they*).
- **Pronoun ambiguity.** A pronoun is only acceptable if it can refer to *exactly one* possible noun. If multiple plural nouns are upstream, the choice with no pronoun usually wins.
- **Personal pronouns for people.** Use *who* (not *that*) for people on the SAT.
- **Who vs whom.** *Who* = subject (replace with *he/she/they*). *Whom* = object (replace with *him/her/them*). Test by rewriting as a question: "Who taught me?" → *he* answers it → use *who*.
- **Possessives:**
  - Possessive pronouns *never* take apostrophes (its, their, your, whose). Apostrophe forms (*it's, they're, you're, who's*) are always contractions of "is/are." Test by un-contracting.
  - Plural noun ending in *-s* → just add `'`. (`the cats' toy`)
  - Every other noun → `'s`. (`the dog's ball`, `the children's game`)
- **Adjectives modify nouns; adverbs modify verbs/adjectives/adverbs.** "I walked *slowly*" (not *slow*).
- **Verb tenses** must produce a logically possible sequence given the passage context.
- **Verb-forms as nouns** (gerund -ing, infinitive to-): can be subjects or objects.
- **(Body of book covers more):** commas, semicolons, colons, dashes, apostrophes for contractions, parallelism, modifier placement, conjunction logic. The *meta-rule* is: the SAT applies these rigidly per its own standards. When unsure, default to the rule even if it sounds stilted.

## VIII. Meta-rules about the test itself

- **Details are critical. Difference between right and wrong can be one word.**
- **Exactly one objectively right answer. No "best" / "better" — only right vs wrong.**
- **The SAT splits hairs. Train to split them too.**
- **Always read the passage introduction.**
- **If you think 2+ choices are equally valid, you missed a detail. If 0 are valid, ditto.**
- **"Most nearly means" / "most likely" / "primarily" → mentally rewrite as "is."**
- **Invest time in easy questions first.** Don't burn your clock on a hard question early.

## IX. What's likely to differ in the 2024 edition (your job to confirm)

Format-driven changes:

1. **Section structure.** 2017 had separate Reading (52q, 65 min) and Writing&Language (44q, 35 min). Digital SAT has one R&W section in two adaptive modules (~27q each, 32 min each). Some pacing strategy from 2017 won't apply.
2. **Passage length.** 2017 used long 500–750-word passages with 10–11 questions each. Digital uses short 25–150-word passages with 1 question each. **Mental-mapping and "skim then citation-first" tactics largely become irrelevant** — there's no map to make.
3. **Question taxonomy.** Digital SAT publishes four content domains: *Information & Ideas, Craft & Structure, Expression of Ideas, Standard English Conventions.* The 2017 question types map roughly:
   - Best evidence → "Command of Evidence (textual/quantitative)" in Info & Ideas.
   - Most-nearly-means / vocab in context → "Words in Context" in Craft & Structure.
   - Central claim / purpose → Craft & Structure.
   - Best accomplishes / transitions / include-exclude → Expression of Ideas.
   - Grammar/style/punctuation → Standard English Conventions.
4. **No essay.** Skip the entire essay chapter (pp. 28506+).
5. **Yes-yes-no-no questions** were a 2017-passage-set artifact. Likely gone; the digital format doesn't have multi-question passages.
6. **Paired passages** are likely gone or restructured, since digital uses single-question stems.
7. **Quantitative evidence questions** (interpret a graph/table to support a claim) may be more prominent on digital.

What should transfer ~unchanged:
- The restate/demonstrate principle.
- All 7 wrong-answer patterns.
- Grammar/style/punctuation rules (mechanical English doesn't change).
- Vocab-in-context heuristic.
- Transitions, sentence-placement, include/exclude logic.
- The four "why most students miss it" reasons.
- The technical-vocabulary glossary.
